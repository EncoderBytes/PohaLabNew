Cell/Cell Organelles

The structural and functional unit of life is the cell. The study of the structure and functioning of cells is known as cell biology. It is concerned with life processes, signalling pathways, physiological qualities, metabolic properties, chemical properties, and cell-environment interactions.

The inside of any animal or plant cell has numerous comparable room-like structures called organelles, which are analogous to the rooms in our homes. Here are some names and explanations of organelles and other cell components.

Cell Wall: Protective Coat in Plant Cells

The presence of a cell wall, which is present solely in plant cells and surrounds the cell membrane, is the most fundamental distinction between plant and animal cells.The cell wall is made of cellulose fibre, polysaccharides, and proteins and is hard. Despite the cell wall's stiffness, chemical messages and cellular excretions can travel across cells.

Cell Membrane: Protective Coat in Animal Cells

The cell membrane is found in both plants and animals, and it is the outermost layer that separates the contents of the cell from the outside world in the animal cell. It is made up of both lipids and proteins and is selectively permeable, which means that only certain molecules may flow through it.

Cytoplasm: Cell’s Inner Space

Cytoplasm is an eighty percent water jelly-like material that is usually transparent in colour. It is also known as cytosol. All of the organelles found within the cell membrane are found in the cytoplasm. The cytosol stores dissolved nutrients, aids in waste breakdown, and transports material around the cell via a process known as cytoplasmic streaming.

Nucleus: The Control Centre

The nucleus is recognised as the cell's command and control core.It contains the regulatory machinery that controls all of the cell's operations. It is the cell's biggest organelle and houses the DNA. DNA includes all of the information necessary for cells to exist, function, and replicate.The nucleus is protected by a double-layered covering known as the nuclear membrane.

Vacuoles: Cell’s Storage Space

A vacuole is a membrane-bound organelle that retains solid and liquid substances. Vacuoles can be found in both animal and plant cells, but they are much larger in plant cells. Vacuoles are generated by the fusing of several membrane vesicles and are essentially just bigger versions of these. The organelle has no fixed shape or size; its structure changes depending on the needs of the cell.

To Prepare Stained Temporary Mount of Onion Peel

Materials Required

Real Lab Procedure

  • Peel off a leaf from half a piece of onion and using the forceps, pull out a piece of transparent onion peel (epidermis) from the leaf.
  • Put the epidermis in the Beaker containing distilled water for 1 minute.
  • Take a few drops of Methylene Blue in a dropper and pour 2-3 drops at the center of a dry glass slide.
  • Using the brush, place the peel onto the slide containing Methylene Blue.
  • Take a cover slip and place it gently on the peel with the aid of a needle.
  • Remove the extra Methylene Blue using a piece of blotting paper.
  • Place this glass side on the stage of the compound microscope and view it.

observations

  • There are a large number of regularly shaped cells lying side by side and each cell has a distinct cell wall.
  • A distinct nucleus is present on the periphery of each cell.
  • Lightly stained cytoplasm is observed in each cell.
  • A large vacuole is present at the centre of each cell, and is surrounded by the cytoplasm.

Conclusion

As cell walls and large vacuoles are clearly observed in all the cells, the cells placed for observation are plant cells.

Precautions

  • Use a brush to transfer the peel from one apparatus to another.
  • Staining of peel should neither be too dark, nor too light.
  • Extra Methylene Blue stain should be removed using blotting paper.
